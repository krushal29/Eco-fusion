Energy

Energy is the fundamental capacity to do work — to move, heat, cool, transform and manufacture. For human societies, energy underpins nearly every activity: it powers homes and hospitals, transports goods and people, supports industry and agriculture, and runs the digital systems that connect modern commerce. Technically, energy exists in many forms (kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical and electrical) and is conserved while being converted between forms through engines, turbines and electrical machines.

Beyond the physics, energy systems shape economies and environments. The type of energy a region uses — whether coal and oil, natural gas, nuclear, hydro, wind or solar — affects air quality, carbon emissions, employment patterns and infrastructure needs. Access to reliable, affordable energy is also a key driver of development: it enables education, healthcare, and small businesses to thrive.

Contemporary energy planning focuses on three intertwined goals: security of supply (reliable availability), affordability (reasonable cost for consumers and industry), and sustainability (minimizing environmental and climate impacts). Meeting these goals requires not only deploying generation resources but also investing in networks, storage, efficiency measures and demand-side management. Technologies like smart meters, batteries and flexible market mechanisms let systems integrate variable renewables while preserving reliability.

This site provides an accessible introduction to the technical concepts, policy choices and practical trade-offs involved in shifting to cleaner, more resilient energy systems, with an emphasis on solar and wind as scalable, cost-effective options for many regions.

Classification of Energy Sources

Energy sources can be classified in multiple, complementary ways. These classifications help planners match technologies to needs and understand the implications for markets, infrastructure and policy.

Primary & Secondary

Primary energy is found in nature: sunlight, wind, flowing water, biomass, coal, oil and natural gas. Secondary energy is the form we actually use after conversion — for example electricity produced from coal or solar panels, or refined fuels like gasoline. Converting primary to secondary energy involves losses and infrastructure; improving conversion efficiency is a major lever for reducing costs and emissions.

Commercial & Non-commercial

Commercial energy is traded on markets and provided by utilities or fuel companies. Non-commercial energy includes locally collected resources used informally (e.g., firewood in rural households). Policies to improve energy access often focus on moving people from non-commercial to modern commercial energy services.

Renewable & Non-renewable

Renewables are naturally replenished on human timescales — sunlight, wind, hydropower, geothermal heat and sustainably managed biomass. Non-renewable resources (coal, oil, gas) form over geological timescales and are finite; their combustion produces greenhouse gases that drive climate change.

Conventional & Non-conventional

Historically, "conventional" referred to large, centralized thermal plants running on fossil fuels. "Non-conventional" (now commonly called "renewable") included distributed and variable technologies like solar and wind. The operational and investment decisions for each group differ, which is why the classification remains useful in planning discussions.

Each classification highlights different priorities — resource mapping and interconnection for renewables, storage and balancing for variable sources, and environmental or social safeguards for large infrastructure projects.

Why are they called non-conventional?

The phrase "non-conventional" stems from the historical dominance of large, centralized power plants using fossil fuels. When alternatives such as small hydro systems, rooftop solar arrays, and community wind projects emerged, they operated under different technical and commercial models and so were labelled "non-conventional." Over time the term evolved into "renewable energy," but the historical distinction remains useful to describe differences in planning and operation.

Several factors explain why non-conventional technologies grew in importance. First, environmental concerns about air pollution and climate change created a strong policy incentive to reduce emissions. Second, technological advances and manufacturing scale reduced costs for solar panels and wind turbines, making them economically competitive. Third, new business models and policy tools (feed-in tariffs, auctions and net metering) provided predictable revenue streams for developers and homeowners.

Non-conventional sources also change how systems are engineered: they introduce variability, decentralization and more actors (households, communities, aggregators). Engineering responses include better short-term forecasting, storage solutions, network reinforcements and market designs that reward flexibility. Social and environmental assessments — for siting, biodiversity and community benefits — are important to ensure sustainable deployment.

In short, the rise of non-conventional technologies reflects a combination of policy choices, technological progress and different societal priorities — and these sources are now central to meeting climate and access goals worldwide.

Current Energy Scenarios  World & India

The global energy landscape in the 2020s is defined by rapid growth in renewables, falling costs for solar PV and wind, and accelerating investment in storage technologies. Policymakers and investors are focused on pathways that meet climate goals while ensuring affordable and reliable energy for consumers.

At a global level, deployment of utility-scale solar and wind has outpaced many expectations, driven by improved supply chains, competitive auctions and corporate procurement. However, the transition also requires retiring or repurposing existing fossil assets, expanding transmission and distribution capacity, and investing in both short- and long-duration storage solutions to manage seasonal variations.

India's energy system is one of the most dynamic. Rapid urbanization and industrialization are increasing electricity demand, while policy targets and auctions have driven sharp growth in solar and wind capacity. The country is also exploring large-scale storage and regional grid integration to balance variable generation. Energy access initiatives are reducing dependence on traditional biomass for cooking and improving reliability in rural areas.

Key elements of future scenarios include technology costs, policy ambition, and financing availability. High-renewable scenarios assume aggressive deployment of solar and wind combined with storage and demand flexibility, while more conservative scenarios rely on a mix including gas and some firm low-carbon generation to manage reliability. The right mix depends on local resources, grids and policy choices.

Solar Energy

Solar energy converts the sun's electromagnetic radiation into electricity or heat. Photovoltaic (PV) panels use semiconductor junctions to generate current when exposed to light, while concentrated solar power (CSP) employs mirrors to focus sunlight and produce heat that drives turbines. The remarkable decline in PV and inverter costs over the past decade has made solar one of the lowest-cost options for new electricity in many regions.

Solar Radiation

The amount of solar energy available at a given site depends on latitude, season, cloud cover and atmospheric conditions. Resource assessment uses historical satellite data and ground measurements to estimate annual insolation and expected yield for specific panel technologies and tilt angles.

Solar is especially powerful because it scales across applications: rooftop PV empowers individual households and businesses; community-scale systems support neighborhoods; and utility-scale farms generate bulk power. Pairing solar with battery storage extends its usefulness into evening and peak hours, and smart inverters provide grid services such as voltage support and frequency response.

Policy instruments (subsidies, auctions, net metering) and corporate procurement have accelerated deployment. Challenges include land use for very large farms, recycling of end-of-life panels, and ensuring equitable access to benefits. Research into perovskites, tandem cells and improved manufacturing promises continued efficiency and cost improvements.

Wind Energy

Wind energy harnesses the kinetic power of moving air to generate electricity via rotor blades and generators. Modern utility turbines are highly engineered machines optimized for high capacity factors and low maintenance. Site choice, hub height and rotor diameter are critical for capturing steady winds and maximizing energy production.

Onshore wind remains one of the lowest-cost sources of new electricity in many regions. Offshore wind, with stronger and more consistent winds, is growing rapidly — especially with the advent of large turbines and floating foundations that open deep-water sites. Modern projects combine advanced resource assessment, digital monitoring and predictive maintenance to improve performance and reduce downtime.

Challenges include integration into grids at high penetration levels, potential local environmental impacts (birds, visual/noise concerns), and the need for transmission upgrades to connect high-resource sites to demand centers. As technology continues to advance, repowering older sites with larger, more efficient turbines can deliver significantly higher yields with the same footprint.
