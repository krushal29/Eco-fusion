Energy

Energy is the ability to do work. It exists in many forms — kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical and more. Our modern society depends on a mix of conventional (fossil) and non-conventional (renewable) energy sources.

Classification of Energy Sources

  • Conventional: Coal, Oil, Natural Gas — non-renewable
  • Non-conventional: Solar, Wind, Hydro, Biomass — renewable
  • Other: Nuclear (low-carbon but non-renewable fuel)

Why are they called non-conventional?

The term "non-conventional" historically referred to sources that weren't part of the dominant fossil-fuel energy system. Today we use "renewable" more often — sources that naturally replenish over human timescales and generally have lower emissions.

Current Energy Scenarios — World & India

Fossil fuel reserves are finite. Estimates vary, but reserves of easily recoverable coal and oil will last only a few decades at current consumption rates. Transition strategies include scaling renewables, improving efficiency, and developing storage and grid flexibility.

In India, renewable deployment has accelerated: large solar parks, distributed rooftop solar, and increased wind capacity are helping diversify the energy mix.

Solar Energy

Solar energy is radiant light and heat from the sun. It can be converted to electricity using photovoltaic (PV) panels or concentrated solar power (CSP). Solar is scalable: rooftop PV for homes and large utility solar farms.

Solar Radiation

Solar radiation depends on latitude, time of year, atmospheric conditions and time of day.

Wind Energy

Wind energy uses the kinetic energy of moving air to turn turbines and generate electricity. Site selection, wind speed distributions and turbine technology determine performance.
